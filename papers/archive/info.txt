Hospital Chatbot for Color-Coded clinical Pathways.
                    The role of Natural Processing Language.


INTRODUCTION
Emergency Departments (EDs) globally face a crisis of overcrowding. A major structural driver of this crisis is the so-called "Green Tag Burden" that is the influx of patients with non-urgent, primary-care-level symptoms who nevertheless arrive at and occupy acute emergency facilities. In low- and middle-income countries (LMICs) such as Ghana, this problem is compounded by chronic understaffing, limited diagnostic equipment, and the near-total absence of pre-hospital triage infrastructure. At the Komfo Anokye Teaching Hospital (KATH) in Kumasi, patients with a wide range of acuity levels compete for the same clinical resources, and under-triage (the dangerous misclassification of a high-acuity patient into a lower priority category) remains a persistent patient safety risk.
This project proposes a digital triage chatbot that acts as a patient's first point of contact before they enter the emergency ward. The chatbot collects information, computes a Triage Early Warning Score (TEWS), and routes each patient to the appropriate colour-coded clinical pathway. The technical architecture of this chatbot rests on three mathematical models working in concert: a Deterministic Algebraic model for objective vital sign scoring, a Probabilistic Bayesian model for handling missing data, and a Deep Attention Natural Language Processing model for interpreting the unstructured text patients use to describe how they feel.
This report focuses on the third model. It addresses a question that the other two models cannot: what happens when a patient does not report their blood pressure or respiratory rate numerically, but instead types, "I woke up and my chest is crushing me. I feel dizzy and I cannot catch my breath"? The numbers are absent, but the clinical emergency is unmistakable — to a trained clinician. The NLP module is what allows the chatbot to read that sentence the way a clinician would.


WHAT IS NATURAL LANGUAGE PROCESSING (NLP).
1.1 Definition and Scope
Natural Language Processing (NLP) is a subfield of artificial intelligence and computational linguistics concerned with enabling computers to understand, interpret, and generate human language in a meaningful way. Unlike structured data: numbers, dates, categorical codes, human language is inherently ambiguous, contextual, and variable. The same clinical concept can be expressed in dozens of different ways: "my chest hurts", "I have chest tightness", "there is pressure in my chest", "my heart feels heavy", and "I cannot breathe properly" may all point toward the same life-threatening presentation of acute coronary syndrome.
NLP bridges this gap by providing computational tools that can parse, interpret, and extract meaning from free-form text. In a clinical context, this means identifying what symptom a patient is describing, how severe they suggest it is, and whether their language maps onto a known clinical discriminator and flags a specific level of medical urgency.

1.2 The NLP Pipeline: From Raw Text to Clinical Meaning.
A clinical NLP system does not simply search for keywords. It processes text through a sequence of analytical steps, each building on the last. The following pipeline describes how a patient's SMS message moves from raw words to a structured clinical signal in this chatbot.

Step 1: Tokenisation
The raw input text is broken into individual units called tokens. A token is most commonly a word, but it can also be a punctuation mark or a subword unit (for example, "breathlessness" may be split into "breath" and "lessness" to handle rare words the model has not seen before). Tokenisation is the first step in converting continuous text into a format a mathematical model can process.

Step 2: Named Entity Recognition (NER)
Once the text is tokenised, the NLP system performs Named Entity Recognition, which involves the automatic identification and classification of medically meaningful terms within the text. In a clinical context, entities include symptoms ("chest pain", "dizziness"), vital sign references ("pulse 125", "breathing 26 times a minute"), anatomical locations ("central", "left arm"), and severity qualifiers ("crushing", "sharp", "mild"). NER is what allows the chatbot to distinguish between the medically significant word "crushing" and the clinically irrelevant word "I" in the same sentence.
For this project, the NER layer is built on BioBERT — a variant of the BERT (Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers) architecture pre-trained on 4.5 billion words from PubMed biomedical abstracts and 13.5 billion words from PubMed Central full-text articles. Because BioBERT has been trained on biomedical literature, it natively recognises domain-specific terms such as "tachycardia", "diaphoresis", "syncope", and "central chest pain" — terms that a general-purpose language model trained on Wikipedia might not correctly identify as clinically significant.

Step 3: Contextual Embedding
After entity recognition, each token is converted into a high-dimensional numerical vector; this is a representation of the word's meaning in context. This process, called embedding, is what allows the model to understand that "crushing" in the context of chest pain carries very different clinical weight from "crushing" in the context of a traffic accident. These embeddings are not fixed dictionaries; they are dynamically computed based on every other word in the sentence, which is what makes transformer-based models so powerful for clinical text.

Step 4: Attention Scoring
With contextual embeddings in place, the deep attention mechanism assigns a numerical importance score(an attention weight) to each token. Tokens whose meaning aligns strongly with known clinical discriminators receive high weights; filler words receive near-zero weights.

Step 5: Discriminator Detection and Classification
The final step maps the weighted clinical representation onto the SATS discriminator taxonomy. SATS defines specific clinical discriminators — symptom-based criteria that immediately determine a minimum triage colour regardless of the numerical TEWS score. If the NLP module detects with sufficient confidence that the patient's text contains a Red Discriminator (for example, central chest pain, altered consciousness, or uncontrolled haemorrhage), this finding overrides all other scores and the patient is immediately assigned to the Red or Orange pathway.


WHY NLP IS NECESSARY FOR THIS PROJECT
1.0 The Limitation of Pure Numerical Triage in a Pre-Hospital Chatbot
The Deterministic Algebraic model (Model 1) is highly reliable when all six TEWS parameters — mobility, respiratory rate, heart rate, temperature, neurological status, and trauma  are known and entered correctly. However, this model has a fundamental operational limitation: it fails entirely when even one vital sign is missing. In a pre-hospital chatbot setting accessed via SMS, this failure is not an edge case, it is the norm.
Most patients texting a hospital chatbot from home do not own a pulse oximeter, blood pressure cuff, or clinical thermometer. They cannot reliably count their own respiratory rate. What they can do is describe how they feel in plain language. A patient experiencing a myocardial infarction will not typically say "my heart rate is 128 beats per minute and my oxygen saturation is 91%". They will say "the pain in my chest is unbearable and it is spreading to my left arm". The NLP module is designed precisely to extract the clinical signal from that kind of statement.


1.1 The LMIC and Ghanaian Context
The necessity of NLP is even more acute in the Ghanaian healthcare context. Implementing triage systems that depend on objective diagnostic measurements; the approach of the Emergency Severity Index (ESI) used in the United States, is practically unworkable in low- and middle-income countries due to critical shortages of nursing staff and prohibitively expensive diagnostic equipment. The SATS framework was adopted precisely because it reduces reliance on advanced diagnostics. The NLP module extends this principle to the digital pre-hospital layer: it allows the chatbot to generate a meaningful triage signal even when the patient has no access to clinical measuring instruments.
Furthermore, patients in the Ghanaian context may express symptoms in non-standard English, in informal registers, or using culturally specific idioms. A study by Luo and colleagues (2022) demonstrated that symptom-centric NLP models trained on formal clinical text can generalise across linguistic registers, successfully identifying symptom mentions even in colloquial and abbreviated language. This generalisation capacity is a prerequisite for any chatbot intended to serve a broad patient population with varying education levels and digital literacy.


1.2 NLP Enables the Hybrid System to Be Safer Than Any Single Model
Each of the three models in the hybrid architecture has a specific weakness. The Deterministic Algebraic model fails on incomplete data. The Probabilistic Bayesian model relies on population-level priors that may not reflect an individual patient's unique presentation. The NLP model, without constraints, risks misinterpreting colloquial language or producing hallucinated clinical conclusions.
The power of the hybrid system is that these weaknesses are complementary, not additive. When the algebraic model is stymied by missing vital signs, the NLP model can provide a directional triage signal from the patient's words. When the NLP model produces an ambiguous result, the Bayesian model can infer the most probable acuity class from available partial evidence. And when the NLP model detects a high-confidence clinical discriminator, it can override both other models with a hard safety rule — ensuring that no patient with language strongly suggestive of a life-threatening emergency is triaged downward by a lower numerical score.


 HOW NLP INTEGRATES WITH THE SATS TRIAGE FRAMEWORK.
1.0 SATS and Its Discriminator Logic
The South African Triage Scale (SATS) assigns patients to one of five colour-coded categories: Red (immediate), Orange (very urgent), Yellow (urgent), Green (routine), and Blue (deceased on arrival). The scale uses two parallel assessment tools: the Triage Early Warning Score (TEWS), derived from six objective physiological parameters, and a set of clinical discriminators; symptom-based criteria that can override the TEWS score to ensure that high-risk presentations are never under-classified.
Clinical discriminators are the SATS mechanism for capturing what numbers cannot always capture. A patient in the early stages of a large myocardial infarction may still have a pulse rate within the normal range, a respiratory rate below the alarming threshold, and a temperature of 37 degrees — producing a TEWS of zero or one. Yet the symptom of central chest pain with radiation to the arm, identified by a trained clinician or by the NLP module of this chatbot, immediately elevates that patient to the Orange pathway regardless of the TEWS score. This is the clinical discriminator override in action.
The NLP module's primary function within the SATS framework is to perform automated discriminator detection: parsing the patient's own words to identify whether their description matches any of the SATS discriminator criteria, and if so, at what confidence level.



1.2 Mapping NLP Output to SATS Colour Pathways
The NLP module's output is not a triage colour directly. It is a set of detected discriminator signals but it's a vector D of binary flags indicating which SATS discriminators have been detected in the patient's text, each with an associated confidence score. These signals are then passed to the master classification logic alongside the TEWS score T and the Bayesian probability distribution.
For example, After processing the Kumasi case study patient's message, the NLP module outputs D = {Central Chest Pain: 0.94, Haemodynamic Compromise: 0.71, Respiratory Distress: 0.68}. These three discriminators all belong to the Orange or Red categories in the SATS taxonomy. The classification logic then applies the hierarchy: since Central Chest Pain is an Orange Discriminator and the patient is conscious, the system assigns C = Orange, alerting the cardiology team and bypassing the standard queue — even though the algebraic TEWS score of 4 would alone have suggested only a Yellow pathway.
This interaction between NLP discriminator detection and algebraic scoring is the core integration mechanism of the hybrid model. The two approaches are not alternatives but they are complementary instruments providing different types of clinical information about the same patient, and their combination produces a routing decision that is both more accurate and safer than either could achieve independently.



1.3 Clinical Guardrails: Preventing Hallucination in Safety-Critical Triage
A critical engineering concern in deploying any NLP or LLM system in a clinical context is the phenomenon of hallucination — the generation of plausible-sounding but factually incorrect or clinically dangerous outputs. In triage, hallucination is not merely an inconvenience: a hallucinated clinical conclusion that down-grades a patient's urgency could cost a life.
Research by Asgari and colleagues (2025) evaluated GPT-4 across 18 experimental configurations for clinical text generation and found a hallucination rate of 1.47 percent per sentence under standard conditions. While this may appear small, it is medically unacceptable in triage: a single hallucinated or omitted discriminator detection could cause a high-acuity patient to be sent to the Green pathway when they need immediate resuscitation.
This project addresses the hallucination risk through two structural mechanisms. First, the NLP module is not a generative model — it does not produce free text. It is a classification model with a fixed, enumerated set of possible output signals mapped to the SATS discriminator taxonomy. This eliminates the open-ended generation risk entirely. Second, the discriminator override rule is deterministic: it is implemented in code as a hard conditional, not as a probabilistic output of the neural network. The neural network's role is to produce confidence scores for discriminator presence; the decision about whether those scores cross the threshold for an override is made by a non-learnable, transparent rule [18]. This architecture is consistent with the guardrail framework proposed by Painter and colleagues (2024), who demonstrated significant reductions in hallucination-related safety errors through the combination of domain-specific fine-tuning and hard "never event" constraints.


REAL WORLD NLP TRIAGE SYSTEM: EVIDENCE FROM PRACTICE.
The NLP approaches described in this report are not theoretical. Several commercially deployed and peer-reviewed systems have demonstrated that NLP-driven triage chatbots can operate with clinical validity in real healthcare settings. The following case studies validate key design decisions made in this project.

1.O Ada Health: NLP for Global Symptom Assessment
Ada Health is a global AI-powered symptom assessment application that uses an NLP-driven conversational engine to collect symptom descriptions from patients and generate triage recommendations. In peer-reviewed benchmarking studies, Ada correctly identified medical conditions with 99 percent accuracy and provided safe triage advice in 97 percent of cases. The system's NLP engine is capable of mapping colloquial symptom descriptions onto standardised medical ontologies, handling multi-symptom presentations, and adapting its questioning strategy dynamically based on prior responses in the conversation.
Ada's performance benchmarks are directly relevant to this project because they were achieved across a linguistically and demographically diverse global user base, including users in low- and middle-income countries with varying education and digital literacy levels. This validates the feasibility of deploying a high-accuracy NLP triage system in the Ghanaian context, provided the conversational interface is appropriately calibrated for the local population.

1.1 Infermedica: NLP in Health System Triage Engines
Infermedica is an AI-powered medical triage engine deployed by health systems across multiple countries. Its NLP component processes patient-reported symptoms in natural language and maps them to a probabilistic diagnostic model. Real-world deployment data showed that Infermedica modified care-seeking behaviour in over 80 percent of interactions, significantly reducing unnecessary emergency care intent by directing low-acuity patients to appropriate outpatient settings. This real-world outcome directly addresses the Green Tag Burden problem that this project targets.
Infermedica's success illustrates an important design principle: the most impactful contribution of the NLP module is not necessarily the identification of the most critical patients — trained clinical staff are already alert to those. The greatest gain is in correctly identifying and redirecting the large volume of low-acuity patients whose presentation does not merit emergency department resources but who, without guidance, default to ED attendance. The NLP module's ability to parse a patient's description of a mild rash, a week-old back pain, or a minor laceration and confidently route them to the Green pathway is as clinically valuable as its ability to detect a cardiac discriminator.


1.2 Gligorijevic et al. (2018): The Academic Benchmark
Beyond commercial systems, the academic work of Gligorijevic and colleagues provides the most rigorous peer-reviewed validation of the deep attention NLP approach that this project adopts. Across 338,500 ED patient visits, their Deep Attention Model demonstrated that combining structured vital sign data with attention-weighted unstructured text produced a 16 percent improvement in triage accuracy over models using structured data alone. The attention weights themselves were shown to be clinically interpretable: the model consistently assigned the highest weights to the same clinical keywords that experienced emergency nurses identified as most salient when manually reviewing cases.
This finding has two implications for this project. First, it validates the core technical approach: the deep attention mechanism does learn to read clinical text the way clinicians read it, and this learning transfers to improved triage accuracy. Second, it establishes interpretability as a measurable property of the model, not just an aspiration. The attention heatmaps generated by the model can be reviewed by supervising clinicians to verify that the chatbot's reasoning is clinically sound capability that is essential for regulatory acceptance and for building clinician trust in an AI triage system.




LIMITATION OF THE NLP MODULE AND FUTURE DEVELOPMENT
1. Current Limitations
Despite its advantages, the NLP module carries limitations that must be acknowledged. The model's discriminator detection performance is bounded by the quality and size of its training corpus. If the model is trained primarily on formal clinical text from high-income country electronic health records, its accuracy on informal Ghanaian patient language may be reduced. Fine-tuning on locally collected patient messages from KATH is therefore not a refinement but a necessity.
The NLP module also cannot verify the accuracy of what a patient reports. A patient who exaggerates symptoms — whether inadvertently, due to anxiety, or intentionally — may receive a higher acuity classification than their physiological state warrants. Conversely, a patient who minimises or fails to mention a critical symptom may receive a lower classification. These are not failures of the NLP algorithm specifically; they are inherent properties of any triage system that relies on self-reported information. The mitigation is the follow-up prompting strategy: the chatbot is designed to ask targeted clarifying questions when the NLP confidence in discriminator presence is moderate rather than high or low.

2. The Agentic AI Future
The current conception of the chatbot treats the NLP module as a classification engine that produces a colour-pathway recommendation. The next stage of development, consistent with state-of-the-art trends in clinical AI [19], is to move from a passive recommender to an active agentic system. In an agentic architecture, the NLP module does not merely output a classification — it triggers downstream actions automatically: updating the patient's electronic health record, sending an alert to the relevant specialist team's dashboard, generating a structured handover note for the receiving clinician, and timestamping the triage event for audit purposes. The NLP module would be the sensory layer of this agentic system — the component that reads the patient's words and translates them into structured clinical actions.



CONCLUSION
Natural Language Processing is not an optional enhancement to the Hospital Chatbot for Color-Coded Clinical Pathways — it is a structural necessity. Without it, the chatbot can only function when patients provide complete, numerical vital sign data, which is an unrealistic expectation in a pre-hospital SMS-based context, particularly in the resource-constrained Ghanaian healthcare environment. With it, the chatbot can extract clinically meaningful information from the words patients actually use, regardless of whether they can measure their own heart rate or temperature.
The deep attention NLP model, formalised through the softmax attention mechanism, provides three distinct capabilities that the other models cannot: it reads unstructured patient language, it identifies SATS clinical discriminators embedded in that language, and it provides an interpretable, auditable record of the reasoning behind each triage decision. When integrated with the Deterministic Algebraic and Probabilistic Bayesian models, it completes a hybrid system that is clinically safer, more robust to missing data, and more responsive to the full range of how patients describe their experiences than any single-model approach.
The evidence from real-world systems — Ada Health, Infermedica, and the academic deep attention model of Gligorijevic and colleagues — confirms that NLP-driven triage is not experimental: it is clinically validated, deployable at scale, and effective at reducing the Green Tag Burden that overwhelms emergency departments in Ghana and globally. The mathematical and architectural choices made in this project's NLP module are grounded in peer-reviewed evidence and consistent with the current state of the art in clinical AI.
